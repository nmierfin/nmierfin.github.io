Those Who Are Afraid to Fail Never Truly Learn

“As I get older, I realize being wrong isn't a bad thing like they teach you in school. It is an opportunity to learn something.” - Richard Feynman

Nicholas Ierfino

Electrical Engineer

A lot of people struggle with what they want to do. Even in my 20s, I hear many of my colleagues and friends contemplate, "I don't know what I want to do with my life". This has never been a problem for me.


At the young age of 12, I was introduced to Make: magazine by happenstance after scrolling through my Facebook feed and coming across an advertisement. I quickly purchased a subscription as it sparked my curiosity at the time. From there my vocation for electrical engineering was born.


By 2014, I had fully enthralled myself in the electrical space having quickly learned important concepts such as ohm's law, kirchoff's law, thevenin's theorem, hysterisis and so on. From there it was not enough to just know the material but I must use it for it's endless applications.


Thenceforth, I bought an arduino and raspberry pi where I soon understood the importance of coding in the electrical engineering profession. Moving on in my high school career, I participated in the Brock mentorship program (a program where a high school student works alongside a professor at Brock University on a science fair project) where I developed an eye tracking software to allow those with complete paralysis (other than eye movement) to move a cursor on the screen using their eyes to communicate. This was done using my new found understanding of C++ with open source libraries such as OpenCV.


Upon my final year of high school, I decided to participate in the Skills Competition in the electrical catagory. This is a province and nation wide competition where students have to participate in activities such as reverse engineering a breadboard, locating and fixing faults on a PCB, using hands on soldering, etc. I ended up winnning the provincial tournament and coming second in the nation wide event.

University

2017-2023

After Graduating High-School with Honours and a cumulative academic average of 97%, I began my university career at the University of Waterloo in September 2017.


Initially, I had enrolled in Electrical Engineering at the University of Waterloo finding much success in my university academic career. I excelled especially in courses such as circuits 1, calculus and linear algebra. I even found my first coop job for the National Research Council (NRC) where I assisted in developing a laser consolidation manufacturing system using Visual Basic. I then proceeded to have a coop job for Dofasco being a database administrator and then Toyota in Toronto where I helped maintain supply chain management software.


Having accomplished so much in so little time it is sometimes hard to remember things that are very important. The one thing for me happened to be my health. Having lived in 5 different cities in a year and a half for coop and school on top of the demanding pressures of academia (especially at the University of Waterloo) I ended up burning out pretty bad right about the end of my last coop term. I became pretty dissolutioned with the university academic system becoming particularly frustrated with student quotas (only a certain percentage of students can be allowed to pass due to availability of upper years). Due to this, I realized that it was time to move onto a different place more suited to my interests. Having taken a year off, I decided to move on to the University of Ottawa.


Now today, after being at the University of Ottawa for close to 2 and a half years, I have sustained the highest academic average in my program and graudated in April of 2023. From here, I have acquired a great range of new skills from computer programming, to advanced mathematics, to circuit theory, to solid state physics, to power production, and to communication theory as well as control systems. My current interests and projects are incredibly thrilling and I hope that you enjoy taking a look at them on this website.
